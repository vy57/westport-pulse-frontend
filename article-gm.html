Pitching General Motors: A Lesson in Macro Debate

Published July 2026

When evaluating legacy automakers in the current macroeconomic climate, the surface-level metrics often mask deeper structural vulnerabilities. In a recent macroeconomic debate focused on future asset price trends, I took the position of pitching General Motors (GM) as a distinct sell.

The Core Sell Thesis

The thesis centers on a convergence of tightening monetary conditions and an aggressive, capital-intensive transition to electric vehicles (EVs). While GM has historically relied on the high margins of their internal combustion engine (ICE) trucks and SUVs, that capital pipeline is facing dual pressures.

First, as the cost of borrowing remains elevated, consumer demand for high-ticket, financed vehicles inevitably cools. The localized affordability data tracked by my Westport API mirrors this broader national reality: when debt is expensive, the appetite for heavy durable goods shrinks.

Future Asset Price Pressures

The debate ultimately hinged on how the market prices in the massive capital expenditures required to retool manufacturing lines. GM is attempting to fund a future EV lineup using the cash flow from a legacy ICE business that is facing macroeconomic headwinds.

If consumer demand wavers due to sustained interest rates, that transition timeline is threatened, making the stock highly vulnerable to downward repricing over the next 18 to 24 months.
