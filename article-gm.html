The $17.5 Billion Moat: How Westport’s 2025 Grand List Proves the Asset Class Won the Post Pandemic Economy

Published July 2026

If you want to understand the true legacy of the post-2020 economy, looking at the Federal Reserve’s inflation charts or national wage growth will only give you half the story. The real consequences are written in the tax ledgers of affluent suburban enclaves. In early 2026, Westport’s Town Assessor Paul Friia finalized the October 1, 2025 Grand List, which calculates the sum of all taxable property in the municipality. The numbers reveal a staggering new reality. The town's total assessed value surged by more than 50 percent, jumping from $11.6 billion to an eye-watering $17.5 billion. This is not a reflection of a moderately hot housing market. It is a monument to the greatest transfer and concentration of wealth in modern American history, proving that asset inflation has permanently pulled up the drawbridge on the middle class. The most glaring piece of evidence is exactly where this new wealth is parked. Within that $17.5 billion valuation, residential real estate skyrocketed by 60 percent compared to the last townwide revaluation five years ago. A baseline leap of that magnitude across an entire municipality means that homes have fundamentally shifted from being places of shelter to impenetrable luxury commodities. My long-held opinion is that the remote work revolution was never going to democratize geography; it was always going to hyper-concentrate wealth into the most desirable school districts. Westport’s data validates this completely. When Manhattan’s executive class realized they only had to commute twice a week, the calculus changed permanently. They took their Wall Street compensation packages and anchored them in Fairfield County, turning the post-pandemic flight to the suburbs into a structural, permanent repricing of the entire region. Yet, there is a dark contradiction embedded in Westport’s local data that highlights the fragility of this asset-heavy economy. While residential property exploded by 60 percent, commercial real estate values rose just 16 percent. Even more revealing, the town's personal property assessments—which measure actual reinvestment by local businesses in equipment, computers, and storefront improvements—ticked up by a microscopic 1.2 percent. These figures perfectly illustrate a bifurcated, two-tier reality. The residents of Westport are growing exponentially richer on paper, but the local commercial ecosystem is merely treading water. We are witnessing the rise of dormitory wealth. Instead of functioning as thriving, interconnected local economies where business investment matches residential growth, affluent towns are increasingly serving as ultra-luxurious holding pens for private capital. This data serves as a death knell for regional housing mobility. Because Connecticut state law dictates that property is assessed at 70 percent of its fair market value, a 60 percent town-wide residential spike essentially eradicates the entry-level home. What was once a stretched but attainable goal for a successful dual-income family has been wiped off the map. When economists debate the national affordability crisis in 2026, they frequently point to elevated interest rates or lagging construction. But we must also point to the engineered exclusivity of these affluent borders. Westport’s 2025 Grand List is hard evidence that the modern economy heavily rewards those who hold assets over those who rely on a paycheck. The music stopped, the asset holders grabbed the seats, and a massive wall of equity has permanently locked everyone else out of the room.
